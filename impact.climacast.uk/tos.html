10. Forecast, warning and data limitations

Weather forecasting is inherently uncertain. Forecasts, warnings, observations and impact estimates can change quickly, and conditions can differ significantly over short distances because of terrain, coastlines, urban effects, drainage, exposure, elevation, river catchments, storm tracks, local convection, soil saturation, infrastructure and the exact timing of weather systems.

ClimaCast may be wrong, delayed, unavailable, incomplete, out of date, affected by third-party outages, affected by API restrictions, affected by bugs, affected by model uncertainty, affected by data-source changes, or affected by human or automated interpretation errors. Information shown as current may still have collection, publication, caching, processing, indexing, moderation, queueing or delivery delays.

Forecasts and warnings are not promises. A high-risk signal does not guarantee that severe impacts will happen at your exact location, and a lower-risk signal does not guarantee that you are safe. Small changes in temperature, wind direction, storm track, rainfall intensity, timing, ground conditions or local infrastructure can change the outcome significantly.

Examples of limitations include warning areas that do not perfectly match your street or route, highly localised impacts that are not captured by regional summaries, flood data that does not cover every type of flooding, model agreement that does not guarantee an outcome, model disagreement that does not remove risk, and maps or polygons that are simplified or approximate.

Flood information can be especially complex. River, sea, groundwater, surface-water, drainage, sewer, reservoir and coastal flooding may be monitored or reported differently, and not every source covers every type of flooding. A property or road can be affected even where no alert appears for that precise place, and some official services may tell you to check local councils for certain flood types.

ClimaCast may use automated systems to classify, summarise or highlight risk. Automation can help with speed and clarity, but it can also misunderstand context, overstate confidence, miss edge cases, duplicate old information or fail to recognise that a source has changed meaning. You should treat automated summaries as an aid, not a final authority.

You must use judgement and check official sources before making decisions that could affect safety, money, property, work, travel, school, events, vulnerable people, pets or business operations. Where the consequence of being wrong would be serious, you should seek suitable expert or official advice rather than relying on ClimaCast.
